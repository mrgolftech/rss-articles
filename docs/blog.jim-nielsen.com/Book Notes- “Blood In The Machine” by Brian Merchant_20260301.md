# Book Notes: “Blood In The Machine” by Brian Merchant

**来源:** [blog.jim-nielsen.com](https://blog.jim-nielsen.com)
**发布时间:** Sun, 01 Mar 2026 19:00:00 GMT
**链接:** https://blog.jim-nielsen.com/2026/book-notes-blood-in-the-machine/

---

<p>For my future self, these are a few of my notes from <a href="https://bookshop.org/p/books/blood-in-the-machine-the-origins-of-the-rebellion-against-big-tech-brian-merchant/a375fa0af7c1e86c?ean=9780316487740">this book</a>.</p>
<hr />
<p>A take from one historian on the Luddite movement:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>If workmen disliked certain machines, it was because of the use that they were being put, not because they were machines or because they were new</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Can’t help but think of AI.</p>
<p>I don’t worry about AI becoming AGI and subjugating humanity.</p>
<p>I worry that it’s put to use consolidating power and wealth into the hands of a few at the expense of many.</p>
<p>The Luddites smashed things:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>to destroy, specifically, ‘machinery hurtful to commonality’ — machinery that tore at the social fabric, unduly benefitting a singly party at the expense of the rest of the community.  </p>
</blockquote>
<hr />
<blockquote>
<p>Those who deploy automation can use it to erode the leverage and earning power of others, to capture for themselves the former earnings of a worker. </p>
</blockquote>
<p>It’s no wonder CEOs are all about their employees using AI: it gives them the leverage. </p>
<p>Respect for the natural rights of humans has been displaced in favor of the unnatural rights of property. </p>
<hr />
<p>Richard Arkwright was an entrepreneur in England.</p>
<p>His “innovation” wasn’t the technology for spinning yarn he invented (“pieced together from the inventions of others” would be a better wording), but rather the system of modern factory work he created for putting his machines to work. </p>
<blockquote>
<p>Arkwright’s “main difficulty”, according to early business theorist Andrew Ure, did not “lie so much in the invention of a proper mechanism for drawing out and twisting cotton into a continuous thread, as in […] training human beings to renounce their desultory habits of work and to identify themselves with the unvarying regularity of the complex automaton.” This was his legacy […] for all his innovation, the secret sauce in his groundbreaking success was labor exploitation. </p>
</blockquote>
<p>Not much has changed (which is kind of the point of the book).</p>
<p>The model for success is:</p>
<ul>
<li>Look at the technologies of the day</li>
<li>Recognize what works and could turn a profit</li>
<li>Steal the ideas and put them into action with unmatched aggression and shamelessness</li>
</ul>
<p>As the author says:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[Impose discipline and rigidity on workers, and adapt] them to the rhythms of the machine and the dictates of capital — not the other way around. </p>
</blockquote>
